# Review Framework

## Purpose

This reference defines a practical expert-review model for project applications, especially teaching-reform and library innovation proposals.

It combines three layers:

- hard gate checks
- weighted scoring rubric
- revision priority model

## A. Hard Gate Checks

These are pass/fail or high-risk items.

1. Eligibility
2. Topic alignment with the current call
3. Non-duplication
4. Required materials complete
5. Matching funds or support commitment stated
6. Timeframe and outputs plausible
7. Recommendation path plausible

If one of these is unclear, it should be shown as a gate risk even if the narrative quality is strong.

## B. Weighted Scoring Rubric

### Generic proposal rubric

| Dimension | Weight | What reviewers actually look for |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Alignment | 15 | Is the proposal clearly inside the funding call or strategic priority? |
| Problem & significance | 15 | Is the real problem concrete and worth solving? |
| Innovation | 15 | Is there a genuine new approach, not just new wording? |
| Feasibility | 20 | Can this team actually execute the plan within time, budget, and conditions? |
| Research basis | 10 | Is there prior practice, data, capability, or team readiness? |
| Outcomes & transferability | 15 | Are outputs clear, useful, and suitable for wider adoption? |
| Compliance & presentation | 10 | Is the application complete, consistent, and reviewer-friendly? |

## C. Revision Priority Model

### P1

Likely to block recommendation or seriously damage funding probability.

Examples:

- topic drifts away from the call
- no matching funds
- category is unrealistic
- proposal reads like an engineering build rather than a teaching-reform study
- implementation path is missing or not credible

### P2

Proposal remains valid but loses competitiveness.

Examples:

- innovation not convincing
- outcomes too generic
- research basis too thin
- no evidence of why this team can deliver

### P3

Clarity and polish issues.

Examples:

- title too long
- wording repetitive
- sections overlap
- reviewer effort too high

## D. Practical Scoring Notes

- A proposal can have a strong idea and still score poorly if execution evidence is weak.
- For teaching-reform proposals, reviewers care more about problem-method-result-service logic than technical novelty alone.
- High innovation with low feasibility should not score high overall.
- If the proposal lacks applicant or budget details, compliance and feasibility should both be discounted.

## E. Methodological Roots

This framework is consistent with common grant and teaching-reform review logic:

- rubric-based peer review
- weighted multi-criteria decision analysis
- CIPP-style evaluation thinking for context, input, process, and product
- Delphi/AHP-style indicator refinement when teams want to formalize weights

For this skill, use the framework pragmatically. Do not over-formalize unless the user asks for a research-method appendix.
